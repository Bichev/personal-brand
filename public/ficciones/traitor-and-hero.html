RESEARCHING THE COINCIDENCES…
The Self-Plagiarizing History ▼

Borges' story turns on a hypothesis impossible to prove and impossible to forget: that the events of history are not original. They have been copied, deliberately, from earlier stories — perhaps by conspirators who understood that a martyrdom must move people, and a play already moves them, so why not splice the one into the other?

If even one event in recorded history is a deliberate copy of a literary work, then no event is safe from suspicion. We will look at every revolution and ask which scene of Shakespeare it most resembles.

Caesar vs Kilpatrick ▼

The story names two assassinations: Julius Caesar, Rome, 44 BCE — as staged by Shakespeare in 1599 — and Fergus Kilpatrick, Dublin, 6 August 1824. The dates of one are real; the dates of the other are written.

Both die at a theatre. Both are warned, and both fail to heed the warning. Both speak last words their killers seem to have rehearsed. Both are loved by the people who kill them.

Theatrical Conspiracy ▼

Kilpatrick was a traitor to the Irish cause; his comrades exposed him in private and faced a problem. To execute him publicly would damage the rebellion; to let him live would damage their honour.

James Nolan, the conspirator with literary tastes, suggested the solution: borrow Shakespeare's choreography for Caesar's death, and let Kilpatrick die as a hero in the script. They wrote anonymous letters to themselves, planted prophecies, hired actors. The audience — Dublin — never knew it was watching a play.

Borges' "Themes" ▼

Borges titles the story Tema del traidor y del héroe. Not The Story of; The Theme of. He treats it as a structure that any number of histories could fit, and offers the Irish version because the year 1824 happens to be the example available to him.

This is Borges' habit: to write the formal skeleton of a fiction rather than its body. The traitor-and-hero theme is not about Kilpatrick. It is about every betrayal, every funeral, every statue erected to someone who did not deserve it.

When Fiction Becomes Fact ▼

There is a stronger reading of the story, available only to those willing to take Borges literally: that the procedure described — deliberately plagiarizing literature into history — has happened, is happening, will happen, and we will never know how much of "the past" was actually a play put on by men who needed the past to say something specific.

Watch the waveforms. Where they lock, someone made them lock. Now ask, of every important event you know, whether its peak might be the same kind of lock — and which earlier play it might be a copy of.

The Plagiarism of Time

On the conspiracy that wrote Dublin out of Shakespeare

"Pensó que la historia hubiera copiado la historia ya es bastante alucinatoria; que la historia copie la literatura, es inconcebible…" — Jorge Luis Borges, Tema del traidor y del héroe (1944)

In 1944 Borges published a four-page story about a man named Ryan, who, in attempting to write the biography of his great-grandfather Fergus Kilpatrick — an Irish revolutionary executed at a Dublin theatre in 1824, instantly canonised as martyr — discovers a sequence of impossible coincidences. The anonymous letter that warned Kilpatrick of his own assassination is copied, almost line for line, from a letter to Julius Caesar in Shakespeare's play. Kilpatrick's last words echo Caesar's. The funeral resembles Antony's oration. Eventually Ryan understands. Kilpatrick was a traitor; his comrades, when they exposed him, decided to stage his death using Shakespeare as a script, so that the Irish cause would not be wounded. Ryan, descended from a traitor whose murder was a play, decides to publish the lie.

The waveforms above are the mathematical signature of that decision. Two timelines, two centuries, drawn as continuous signals; everywhere except at five locked instants they wander into different shapes. At those five instants they peak together — not by chance, but because someone made them. Phase-locking is the formal fingerprint of a copy. If two signals are uncorrelated everywhere except at a handful of identical maxima, then someone, somewhere, has spliced the same word into both their scores. Coincidence has a smooth statistical shape; conspiracy has sharp ones. The peaks above are sharp.

Borges' suggestion, half-serious and half-horrified, is that history may have been doing this for a very long time. Gauchos who recite the Iliad, generals who restage Pharsalus, riots whose chants are quoted from the Aeneid — what if the imitation is not metaphor but installation? What if the men who run history have been reading the same plays as the men who write it, and have decided, in private, to make the second performance louder than the first? The shape of every revolution, on this reading, is the shape of an earlier play someone admired enough to forge.

Ryan publishes the lie. He has no choice; the lie has been authored too well. And we are left watching the two centuries breathe in time. If you type any phrase into the oracle, it will appear as a third locked instant on both lines — a third "coincidence" you yourself have just authored. The piece does not ask whether history has plagiarized literature. It asks, more disquietingly, whether the difference still means anything. If every important moment rhymes with an earlier important moment, then we are not living forward. We are reading. The author has merely changed costumes.

⬡ The Literary Heart

"Si la historia ha copiado a la literatura, es inconcebible."

"That history may have copied literature is inconceivable."

y(t · 1599) ≅ y(t · 1824) ∀ t ∈ { t₁, t₂, t₃, t₄, t₅ }

Two timelines, two centuries; their underlying signals are uncorrelated almost everywhere — but they peak identically at five locked instants. That locking is the formal signature of a deliberate copy: independent noise plus identical maxima plus a witness who notices. The witness is Ryan. The witness is you.
